Buying a home is one of the largest financial decisions you'll make. It's also a process built on a hundred small phrases — most of them inherited, abbreviated, or written for solicitors rather than for you.

This glossary is our way of putting that right. We've grouped the terms by the stage of the journey they belong to, so you can read them in order or jump to the part you're standing in now. If anything still feels unclear, that's exactly what your advisor is here for — one conversation will save you a dozen searches.

## Before you make an offer

**Vendor** — the person or party selling the property.

**Memorandum of Sale** — the document confirming buyer, seller and agreed price once an offer is accepted. It kicks off the legal process.

**Subject to Contract (STC)** — an offer status meaning the price is agreed in principle, but neither side is committed until contracts are exchanged.

**Under Offer** — the seller has accepted an offer; exchange of contracts has not yet happened.

**Chain** — a sequence of linked sales where each transaction depends on the next. Long chains are a common cause of delay.

**Chain-free** — neither buyer nor seller depends on another sale completing first. The fastest possible position to be in.

**Gazumping** — when a seller accepts a higher offer from a different buyer after already agreeing yours. Legal in England and Wales until contracts are exchanged, which is why exchange matters.

**Gazundering** — when a buyer drops their offer shortly before exchange, hoping the seller accepts the lower figure rather than restart.

**Agreement in Principle / Decision in Principle (AIP/DIP)** — also called a Mortgage in Principle. A statement from a lender showing what they would lend you if you passed full checks. Useful at the offer stage as proof of buying power.

**New Build Premium** — the extra often paid for a brand-new property versus an equivalent older home. Sometimes justified by warranties and finish; sometimes not.

**Part Exchange** — a scheme offered by some developers where they buy your current home so you can move into a new build without a chain.

## Mortgages and money

**Mortgage** — a loan secured against the property. The lender registers a "first charge" so the home can't be sold without their loan being repaid.

**Mortgage Advance** — the amount of money the lender agrees to lend you.

**Mortgage Term** — the period over which you'll repay the loan, typically 25 to 35 years.

**Repayment mortgage** — a standard mortgage where each monthly payment covers both interest and a portion of the capital. At term end the loan is fully cleared.

**Interest-only mortgage** — your monthly payment covers only the interest. The full borrowed amount remains due at the end of the term, repaid from savings, investments or the sale of the property.

**Loan to Value (LTV)** — the percentage of the purchase price covered by the mortgage. A lower LTV usually means a better interest rate.

**Standard Variable Rate (SVR)** — the lender's default interest rate, broadly tracking the Bank of England base rate. Most fixed deals roll onto SVR when they end, often at a higher cost.

**Base Rate** — the interest rate set by the Bank of England. Changes here ripple into mortgage rates over time.

**Early Repayment Charge (ERC)** — a fee charged if you repay or switch your mortgage before the agreed term ends. Always check the small print on fixed deals.

**Overpayment** — paying more than your scheduled monthly amount. Most lenders allow up to 10% of the balance per year before ERCs apply.

**Portable Mortgage** — a mortgage that can move with you to a new property, subject to the lender's approval. Helpful if you bought into a low rate.

**Redemption Statement** — the lender's formal figure for what it would cost to fully clear your mortgage on a given date.

**First Charge** — the priority claim a mortgage lender has against the property. Their debt is repaid first if the property is sold.

**Guarantor** — someone who agrees to take over the mortgage payments if the borrower can't. Often a parent, especially for first-time buyers.

**Equity / Negative Equity** — your equity is the property's value minus what you owe against it. Negative equity is when the loan is larger than the home's current value.

**Equity Loan** — a loan, often from a government scheme, sized as a percentage of the purchase price and repaid at that same percentage of the eventual sale price.

**Bridging Loan** — short-term finance that bridges the gap between buying a new property and selling your existing one. Useful but typically expensive.

**Arrears** — being overdue on payments, most commonly mortgage payments. Speak to your lender early; options are widest before they're enforced.

**Mortgage Broker** — a specialist who shops the market for a suitable mortgage. Some charge a fee, others are paid by the lender.

**Lifetime ISA** — a UK savings product for first-time buyers under 40, with up to £4,000 paid in per tax year and a 25% government bonus on top.

**Help to Buy (historic)** — the closed government scheme that offered an equity loan toward a new build. Worth knowing if you bought under it, as the loan terms still apply.

## Surveys and searches

**Valuation** — an estimate of what the property is worth. May be a quick estate agent appraisal, an online instant valuation, or a formal survey.

**Valuation Survey** — the lender's check on the property's value and lendable condition. It protects them, not you.

**Home Buyers Report** — a mid-level condition report by a qualified surveyor. Suitable for most conventional homes in reasonable condition.

**Building Survey** — the most detailed structural inspection. Recommended for older, larger or unusual properties.

**Snagging Survey** — an inspection of a new build to identify defects and unfinished work before completion. Catch them while the developer is still obliged to fix.

**RICS** — the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors. A RICS-accredited surveyor is the professional standard for any of the above.

**NHBC Scheme** — the National House-Building Council's warranty programme covering most new builds in the UK against major defects.

**Energy Performance Certificate (EPC)** — rates the property's energy efficiency from A to G. Valid for ten years.

**Local Authority Search** — confirms what the council knows about the property: planning history, restrictions, road schemes nearby.

**Drainage and Water Search** — confirms the home is connected to mains water and drainage, and locates public pipes.

**Environmental Search** — flags risks such as flooding, landfill, contaminated land or nearby industrial use.

**Mining Search** — required in areas with mining history; checks for ground stability risks.

**Indemnity Insurance** — a one-off policy that protects against a specific legal risk found during conveyancing (such as a missing planning consent) when the cost or time to resolve it properly would be greater.

**Retention** — when a lender holds back part of the mortgage advance until specific repairs or works are completed.

## Legal and conveyancing

**Conveyancing** — the legal process of transferring ownership of property from one party to another.

**Solicitor's Fees (Legal Fees)** — what you pay your solicitor or licensed conveyancer for their work on the purchase.

**Disbursements** — third-party costs paid through your solicitor: searches, Land Registry fees, bank transfers and the like.

**Land Registry** — the central government database of property ownership in England and Wales. Every sale is registered here.

**Title Plan** — the Land Registry's map of the property's boundaries.

**Deeds** — the original documents recording ownership, boundaries, mortgages and any rights or restrictions on the land.

**Report on Title** — your solicitor's summary of everything they've found, written for you to sign off before exchange.

**Easement** — a legal right one party has over another's land — typically a right of way or to maintain pipes and cables.

**Covenants** — rules or obligations attached to the title. May be restrictive (what you can't do) or positive (what you must do).

**Restrictive Covenant** — a specific limitation on how the property may be used, for example no extensions or no commercial activity.

**Caveat Emptor** — Latin for "buyer beware". In English property law, it's your responsibility to check the property's condition; the seller isn't obliged to volunteer faults.

**Power of Attorney (PoA)** — formal authority to act on someone else's behalf in legal or financial matters. Useful if a buyer or seller can't sign in person.

**Deed of Variation** — a legal document amending the terms of a lease — for example, correcting a clause or adjusting ground rent.

**Exchange of Contracts** — the moment signed contracts are swapped between solicitors. From here, both parties are legally bound.

**Exchange and Completion Gap** — the window between exchange and completion, anywhere from same-day to several weeks depending on the chain.

**Completion** — the day the money is transferred, the keys are released and you become the legal owner.

**Completion Statement** — your solicitor's final breakdown of all monies in and out at completion.

**Vacant Possession** — the requirement that the seller (and any tenants) have moved out, with the property empty, on the day of completion.

**Fixtures and Fittings** — the items the seller is including in or excluding from the sale. Confirmed in writing on the TA10 form before exchange.

## Ownership types

**Freehold** — outright ownership of the property and the land it stands on. You're responsible for the upkeep.

**Leasehold** — you own the right to live in the property for a fixed number of years, with a freeholder owning the underlying land.

**Length of Lease** — the number of years left on a leasehold. Short leases (typically under 80 years) are harder to mortgage and more expensive to extend.

**Share of Freehold** — the freehold is jointly owned between the leaseholders of the building. Generally a stronger position than a standard leasehold.

**Commonhold** — a less common alternative to leasehold for flats, where each owner has freehold over their unit and joint responsibility for common areas.

**Flying Freehold** — a part of a freehold property that overhangs land or property owned by someone else. Worth a surveyor's eye and a chat with your lender.

**Joint Tenants** — a form of co-ownership where, on death, the deceased owner's share passes automatically to the other owners.

**Tenants in Common** — a form of co-ownership where each owner has a defined share, which forms part of their estate on death rather than passing automatically.

**Shared Ownership** — a leasehold scheme where you buy a percentage of the home and pay rent on the rest. A common route for first-time buyers.

**Staircasing** — buying additional percentages of a Shared Ownership property over time, with the goal of owning 100%.

## Leasehold specifics

**Ground Rent** — a regular payment from the leaseholder to the freeholder. On most new leases, government reforms have capped or removed this.

**Ground Rent Review Period** — the clause in the lease setting out when and how the ground rent can rise.

**Peppercorn Rent** — a nominal, token rent — historically a single peppercorn — used to keep a lease legally valid without imposing a real cost.

**Service Charges** — regular payments toward shared costs: buildings insurance, cleaning, heating, lifts, caretaking, lighting.

**Sinking Fund / Reserve Fund** — money held by the freeholder or managing agent for larger, periodic works such as a roof or lift replacement.

**Common Parts / Common Areas** — the shared areas of a leasehold building such as hallways, staircases and gardens.

**Management Company / Managing Agent** — the organisation responsible for the building's common parts and for collecting service charges.

## Costs, taxes and ratings

**Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT)** — the government tax payable on most property purchases in England and Northern Ireland above the price threshold in force at the time. (Scotland uses Land and Buildings Transaction Tax; Wales uses Land Transaction Tax.)

**Council Tax Band** — the band (A to H in England) that sets your local council tax bill, based on the property's notional value on 1 April 1991.

**Appreciation / Depreciation** — the rise or fall in a property's value over time.

**Listed Building** — a property protected for its special architectural or historic interest. Listing carries duties — and limits — on what you can change.

## Special categories

**Buy-to-Let** — a property purchased to be let out rather than lived in. Different mortgage products, different tax treatment.

**Affordable Housing** — homes built by Housing Associations or Registered Providers with government subsidy, available below open-market prices or rents.

**Housing Association** — a not-for-profit organisation that builds and manages affordable homes.

**Registered Provider (RP)** — the current term for what used to be called a Registered Social Landlord. Includes most housing associations.
